Distributable Earnings Quality Is the Only Stable Valuation Driver—Scale Alone Does Not Explain the Premium

Across a universe of 23 publicly listed alternative asset managers spanning $35.8B to $1,274.9B in AUM, this analysis identifies which operational and financial characteristics are most associated with valuation multiples—and translates those findings into a strategic playbook for PAX.

Governing finding: Distributable earnings per share (DE/share) is the only stable value driver across the three valuation multiples tested, with a Spearman rho of 0.86 against EV/FEAUM (p = 0.014, n = 7). AUM and FEAUM scale correlate with P/DE but not consistently across other multiples. The market appears to reward the quality and predictability of earnings—not merely the magnitude of assets under management.

Key Findings

  • DE/share is the only stable value driver (DVR-001), satisfying the stable_v1_two_of_three criterion with |rho| ≥ 0.5 on two of three multiples. Firms that generate higher distributable earnings per share command higher EV/FEAUM and P/FRE multiples, regardless of absolute AUM scale.1
  • AUM and FEAUM scale are multiple-specific drivers (DVR-003, DVR-004), correlating with P/DE (rho = 0.60) but not with P/FRE or EV/FEAUM. Scale matters for total earnings-based valuation, but it does not independently drive the market's assessment of per-unit-of-AUM economics.
  • FRE growth exhibits a counterintuitive negative relationship with P/DE (DVR-002, rho = -0.75), suggesting that firms experiencing rapid FRE growth may be in earlier-stage scaling phases where the market prices in execution risk rather than rewarding momentum.
  • The most replicable plays for PAX center on fee durability—scaling perpetual capital vehicles, exploiting the LatAm infrastructure deficit, and growing the GP Management Solutions (GPMS) franchise—rather than attempting to emulate the credit-insurance integration model that requires capabilities and balance-sheet scale PAX does not currently possess.
Therefore: PAX's most direct path to valuation re-rating appears to run through FEAUM quality improvement—converting its infrastructure and credit franchises into perpetual or semi-permanent fee-earning structures, scaling GPMS as a uniquely defensible LatAm franchise, and maintaining premium fee rates rather than pursuing AUM aggregation through low-fee products.

Interpreting the Results: Earnings Quality Over Earnings Growth

The finding that DE/share is the sole stable driver carries a specific strategic implication: the market appears to value the level and predictability of per-share distributable earnings more than the rate of change of fee-related earnings or the absolute magnitude of assets under management. This is consistent with the observation that PGHN (Partners Group), with only $174B AUM, commands a premium EV/FEAUM multiple through 107 bps blended fee rates and 64% EBITDA margins—while BAM, at $1,200B AUM, carries a lower DE/share ($1.45) due to fee-rate dilution from rapid credit/insurance scaling.

The negative P/DE correlation for FRE growth (DVR-002, rho = -0.75) is counterintuitive but interpretable. Firms experiencing rapid FRE growth are often in mid-cycle build phases—integrating acquisitions, deploying new product platforms, or scaling credit operations—where the market discounts near-term earnings for execution risk. The FRE growth signal becomes valuation-positive only after the market gains confidence in sustainability, at which point it is captured through DE/share improvement.

What not to overread: FRE margin (DVR-006) shows a weak negative P/FRE correlation (rho = -0.46), which does not indicate that margins are irrelevant. Rather, it suggests that higher-margin firms tend to trade at lower P/FRE multiples because their FRE per unit of market cap is already elevated—a mathematical artifact rather than a strategic signal. Margins remain critical as an input to DE/share.

Only a Subset of Admired Firms Built Premium Through Mechanisms Transferable to PAX

Eight peers were selected for deep profiling based on their explanatory power across the top-ranked valuation drivers. The selection includes rule-confirming leaders (BX, KKR, APO), relevant comparables at different scale points (ARES, EQT, OWL), a margin-quality exemplar (PGHN), and the dominant infrastructure platform (BAM). Each profile identifies the primary mechanism through which the firm's business model connects to the identified valuation drivers.

The largest listed alt manager. Four segments (PE 37%, Credit/Insurance 39%, RE 28%, Multi-Asset 8%) provide diversification that smooths earnings cycles. DE/share of $4.64 and 57% FRE margin reflect operating leverage on $830.7B FEAUM. Perpetual capital doubled from ~15% (2019) to 31% (2024), underpinning fee-stream stability.

Transferability to PAX: Limited. BX's scale advantages (brand, multi-product allocation, BREIT distribution) are not replicable at PAX's size. The perpetual capital thesis, however, is asset-class-agnostic and relevant.

KKR — KKR & Co. $744.0B AUM

Each Asset Class Presents Distinct Pathways to the Valuation Drivers—Infrastructure and Credit Offer PAX the Highest Leverage

Five asset classes were analyzed for market landscape, peer positioning, fee economics, and PAX relevance. The analysis maps each asset class to the ranked valuation drivers to identify where PAX's existing capabilities and geographic positioning create the strongest link between asset class strategy and valuation improvement.

Private Equity

TAM: $8.2T · 11% CAGR

The original alternative asset class. Global PE AUM of $8.2T is growing at 11% CAGR, but mega-fund concentration is reducing the addressable market for mid-scale managers. Continuation vehicles and GP-led secondaries ($150B+ estimated market) represent a structural shift from traditional fund-raise-deploy-exit cycles. Retail democratization through semi-liquid and evergreen PE products (KKR K-Series at $35B+, PGHN evergreen at 32% of AUM) is opening access beyond institutional LPs.

Key peers: BX (flagship buyouts), KKR (PE + growth), EQT (European PE), CG (diversified global PE).

Fee economics: Buyout funds at 100–200 bps management fees with 20% carry. Growth equity at 150–200 bps. Secondaries at 80–120 bps. Highest fee rates in alternatives, but vintage-dependent fundraising creates lumpy fee activation.

PAX relevance: PAX's PE franchise (~$12B AUM) operates across LatAm buyout and growth equity (iGah3 acquisition). The mega-fund concentration trend limits PAX's ability to compete for mega-cap transactions, but the LatAm mid-market remains underserved. Growth equity integration through iGah3 follows the pattern observed at BX Growth, KKR Next Gen Tech, and EQT Growth.

Infrastructure

TAM: $1.5T · 16% CAGR

The fastest-growing private markets asset class at 16% CAGR. LatAm's estimated $150B annual infrastructure investment deficit creates structural deal flow. Three mega-trends—energy transition, digitalization, and deglobalization/nearshoring—converge to drive sustained capital demand. Infrastructure assets have the longest useful lives (20–50+ years), making them natural candidates for perpetual capital vehicles that directly link to DVR-001 (DE/share) and DVR-004 (FEAUM).

Key peers: BAM (largest infrastructure investor globally, $350B+ real assets), EQT (full-spectrum infra), KKR (GII series ~$80B), ANTIN (pure-play infra specialist).

Fee economics: Equity infrastructure at 60–90 bps management fees with 15–20% carry. Infrastructure credit at 75–125 bps with 10–15% carry. Core/core+ open-end vehicles at 50–75 bps with perpetual fee duration.

PAX relevance: Infrastructure (~$15B AUM) is characterized as PAX's "crown jewel" franchise. Leading LatAm infrastructure position with captive origination in Brazil's concession market provides a defensible moat against global entrants who lack regulatory expertise, local relationships, and currency management experience. The absence of an open-end or evergreen infrastructure vehicle represents the single highest-impact product gap. An infrastructure credit capability could bridge infrastructure and credit platforms, capturing both equity and debt allocations.

Credit

TAM: $3.5T · 14% CAGR

The dominant growth engine for alt-manager AUM and FRE. Private credit AUM of $3.5T with 14% CAGR, driven by bank retrenchment, insurance demand, and yield-seeking institutional capital. Credit platforms drive FRE growth (DVR-002) through continuous fundraising and fee activation without the J-curve effect of PE drawdown funds. LatAm private credit is structurally attractive: high nominal yields (Selic at 13.75%+), low competition from global mega-managers due to currency/regulatory barriers, and secular bank retrenchment.

Key peers: APO (credit at $749B, 80%+ of AUM), ARES (credit at $406.9B, 65% of AUM), OWL (direct lending via BDCs, 55% credit).

Fee economics: Direct lending at 100–150 bps management fees. CLO management at 40–50 bps. Investment-grade private credit at 20–35 bps. Higher fee rates in direct lending and special situations offset by lower rates in scaled insurance/IG strategies.

PAX relevance: PAX's credit platform (~$8B AUM) appears concentrated in drawdown structures. A listed credit vehicle (FIDC or similar CVM-regulated structure) could provide a BDC-analog in the LatAm market. LatAm's high-rate environment creates attractive yield spreads, and secular bank retrenchment opens origination supply. Scaling credit from $5B to $15B+ FEAUM by 2028 is the primary FRE growth lever.

Real Estate

TAM: $4.5T · 9% CAGR

The most mature alternative asset class. Global private RE AUM of $4.5T growing at 9% CAGR. Logistics/industrial and living sectors have replaced office as the institutional allocation preference. Non-traded REITs and perpetual vehicles (BX BREIT at $60B+) have transformed the capital formation model. Brazil's FII market surpassed R$200B AUM with 2M+ retail investors, establishing a scaled listed vehicle ecosystem.

Key peers: BX (largest private RE, BREIT), EQT/Exeter ($34B US industrial RE), BAM (global real estate), OWL (net lease/RE credit).

PAX relevance: PAX's RE platform (~$3.5B AUM) is the smallest major segment. Rather than competing on scale, leveraging Brazil's FII infrastructure for logistics and industrial themes, and selectively exploring Mexico nearshoring-driven industrial RE, could provide capital-efficient growth. GPMS provides indirect RE exposure through stakes in RE-focused GPs, bypassing the need to scale the direct platform to globally competitive levels.

GP Stakes / Management Solutions

TAM: ~$200B+ · emerging

An emerging meta-asset-class where managers acquire minority stakes in other GPs, creating a revenue stream that compounds with the growth of the broader alternatives industry. GP stakes generate the highest fee durability—perpetual, non-redeemable management fee participation at 55–65% FRE margins with near-zero marginal operating cost. The asset class is dominated by OWL (GP Strategic Capital ~$65B) with newer entrants including BX Strategic Capital (~$10B) and KKR/Arctos.

Key peers: OWL (category-defining, 40+ GP relationships), BX (ecosystem model), KKR/Arctos (mid-market GPs).

PAX relevance: GPMS (~$1.5B, contributing $6–7B to pro-forma FEAUM through partner GP AUM) is the first dedicated GP stakes program in Latin America, operating without direct regional competition. GPMS may be PAX's most strategically important initiative from a valuation perspective: it provides exposure to diversified LatAm alternative asset economics through a single product, generates perpetual-capital-like fee durability, and creates network effects as portfolio GPs become distribution partners. Scaling toward $5–10B over the next fundraising cycle appears to be the highest-quality revenue opportunity available to a mid-scale alt manager.

While transformative M&A drives AUM step-changes, serial acquisitions without integration compress FRE margins, dilute culture, and create operational complexity. Integration costs consume 2–4 years of margin headroom.

ANTI-004 Medium Confidence

ANTI-005 Medium Confidence

The Peer Analysis Is Directionally Robust, but Causal Claims Should Remain Hedged

Universe Construction

The peer universe comprises 23 publicly listed alternative asset managers selected based on: (1) >50% of revenue or strategic identity derived from alternative asset management, (2) public listing on a major exchange with audited financial disclosures, and (3) relevance to PAX's business model, scale range, or strategic trajectory. Eight firms were excluded due to delisting (AMK, PHL), acquisition (SCU), non-listing (PAG), or structural non-comparability (3i, BN, BNT, BIP).

Metric Framework

Thirty-one metrics were cataloged across 9 categories: earnings (3), scale (4), organic growth (2), mix/diversification (3), efficiency (3), fee quality (2), operational feasibility (8), valuation multiples (3), and market structure (3). Twenty-five metrics were designated as driver candidates; 3 valuation multiples served as dependent variables; 3 market structure metrics were contextual-only.

Limitations

  • Correlation, not causation. Cross-sectional rank correlation identifies statistical associations but cannot establish causal relationships. The finding that DE/share correlates with EV/FEAUM does not prove that improving DE/share will mechanically increase EV/FEAUM.
  • Small sample sizes. P/DE correlations are based on as few as 5–6 firms due to limited DE disclosure among non-US-listed managers. Only one correlation (COR-001: DE/share vs. EV/FEAUM, p = 0.014) achieves statistical significance after FDR correction.
  • Non-GAAP heterogeneity. Distributable earnings, FRE, and FEAUM are non-GAAP measures with significant definition differences across firms. Compensation treatment, performance fee inclusion, and tax adjustments vary. Cross-firm comparisons should be interpreted with this caveat.
  • Survivorship bias. The universe includes only currently listed firms. Managers that failed, were acquired, or went private are excluded, potentially biasing toward strategies that happened to succeed during this period.
  • Point-in-time analysis. Correlations reflect a single cross-section (FY2024/H1 2025 data). The relative importance of drivers may shift across market cycles.
  • Currency effects. AUM and earnings figures for non-USD-reporting firms were converted at period-end exchange rates. FX movements may distort cross-firm comparisons, particularly for PAX (BRL-denominated operations, USD reporting).
